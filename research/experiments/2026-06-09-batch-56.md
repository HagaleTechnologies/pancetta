## Headline

Batch 53 graduated LLR whitening to default-ON based on Fast/Moderate tier preset measurement (`max_decode_passes = 2, ldpc_iterations = 200`). Batch 56 verifies the lift extends to the Slow-tier preset (`max_decode_passes = 1, osd_depth = Some(1)`) — the FTdx10 MiniPC production target.

**Result**: Whitening lift is **larger at Slow tier than at Fast tier** in absolute TPs (+14 vs +4), with a smaller but consistent precision lift. The graduation extends to all tiers; no tier-conditional flipping needed.

## Cross-tier comparison

| Tier | Decodes | Baseline TPs | Whitening TPs | Δ TPs | Δ FPs | Baseline Precision | Whitening Precision |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Fast (Batch 53) | 22365→21656 | 16365 | 16369 | **+4** | **−713** | 0.7317 | 0.7559 (+3.3%) |
| Slow (Batch 56) | 18099→18105 | 16226 | 16240 | **+14** | **−8** | 0.8965 | 0.8970 (+0.06%) |

The two tiers have **asymmetric whitening response**:

- **Fast tier**: precision-dominant lift. The multi-pass + deeper OSD generates many FPs (precision 0.7317); whitening primarily removes them (−713). TP lift is modest (+4).
- **Slow tier**: recall-dominant lift. The single-pass + shallow OSD already filters aggressively (precision 0.8965); whitening recovers more borderline TPs (+14) without inflating FP count (−8).

Both tiers show **TPs ↑ AND precision ↑**. The lift is structurally consistent — whitening helps the BP/OSD distinguish signal from noise — but the dominant effect depends on the tier's baseline FP/TP balance.

## Decision

**Default-ON is correct across all tiers.** No tier-conditional flipping needed. The Batch 53 graduation extends cleanly.

Implication for the tier preset logic in `coordinator/tier.rs`: the Slow-tier rewrite (`max_decode_passes = 1, osd_depth = Some(1)`) inherits `llr_whitening_enabled = true` from the default Ft8Config since Batch 53's flip. No change needed.

## Strategic implications

1. **LLR whitening's value is tier-agnostic.** The mechanism's per-tone × per-symbol noise normalization helps the BP/OSD regardless of how many passes or how deep the OSD search. This is unusual — most mechanisms are tier-sensitive (e.g., Fast benefits from extra recall mechanisms because it has the CPU budget for additional passes; Slow can't afford them).

2. **The tier-asymmetric effect (precision-dominant Fast, recall-dominant Slow) is itself informative.** It suggests whitening operates closer to the LDPC decode boundary than the BP iteration count or OSD depth. The mechanism normalizes the LLR input distribution, which the LDPC decoder treats consistently regardless of how many iterations it runs.

3. **Slow tier's baseline precision (0.8965) is much higher than Fast tier's (0.7317).** This makes sense given the design: Slow gives up recall for precision. The hard_1000 corpus has many borderline-SNR signals that Fast tier's multi-pass + deeper OSD finds at the cost of admitting FPs. Slow filters aggressively; whitening lets Slow recover some of the borderline TPs without lowering its precision.

4. **The graduation extension closes a tier-conditional question** that would have been a Batch 57 follow-up. Removes the need for tier-aware default flipping logic, which simplifies `coordinator/tier.rs` (no `tier_dependent_defaults` map needed).
