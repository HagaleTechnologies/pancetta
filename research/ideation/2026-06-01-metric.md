## Context

Pancetta's current composite:

```
score = 0.50 × decode_rate(curated-hard-200)
      + 0.30 × snr_50pct(synth-clean)        // normalized
      + 0.15 × pass_rate(fixtures)
      + 0.05 × snr_50pct(synth-doppler)      // normalized
```

This declares **per-slot recall against jt9 truth on a curated 200-WAV hard
sample as the headline axis** (50% of the composite, 5× any other tier).
Recent observations stress-test that choice:

- **Saturation against hard-200**: 11 graduations in 6 days; recent wins are
  +0.000292 composite — the recall ceiling is bending the curve flat
  (`research/experiments/2026-05-30-corpus-survey.md`).
- **Corpus self-bias**: pancetta-vs-jt9 ratio is 123.7% on hard-200
  (curated *by* pancetta) but only 93% on a fresh K5ARH 20m capture. The
  metric we optimize against is a metric *built from our own decoder*.
- **Operational mismatch**: pancetta's purpose is on-air QSOs. The priority
  scorer in `pancetta-qso/src/priority.rs` weights **needed-DXCC 0.35 +
  needed-grid 0.20 + POTA/SOTA 0.15 + rarity 0.10 + signal 0.05**. None of
  these are uniformly distributed across decoded callsigns — yet the
  composite treats every recovered decode as worth `1/N_truth`.
- **Latency-blind**: a CQ decoded at T+13s of a 15s slot may be too late
  to TX a response in the next slot. Recall says yes; on-air says no.
- **Precision-blind**: the FP filter exists and is enforced as a gate
  (`fp_filter_active: true`) but not *measured* in the composite. A 0-FP
  decoder that recalls 50% scores identically to a 0-FP decoder that
  recalls 50% AND a hypothetical 0.1-FP decoder that recalls 51%.

This file generates 15 alternative metric axes that would, individually or
in combination, change which hypotheses look attractive and which look like
overfit-to-hard-200.

## M1 — Time-to-first-decode (TTFD) per QSO target

**Definition.** For each slot, instead of `decoded_count / truth_count`,
measure `mean(t_first_decode_emit - slot_start_wallclock)` over decoded
truths, where `t_first_decode_emit` is the wallclock instant the decoder
first emits a given truth's text. Aggregate across slots as median TTFD in
seconds. Lower is better. Define a normalized `ttfd_score = clamp((15.0 -
median_ttfd_s) / 15.0, 0, 1)`.

**Why this would change behavior.** Multipass and joint-pair-retry add
decodes that arrive late in slot processing. Under recall, they're free
wins; under TTFD they cost. hb-079 (coherent iterative-subtract multi-pass,
biggest project win) likely *regresses* TTFD because the decodes recovered
on pass 2+ arrive later. hb-091 (a8-style early-decode) jumps from 0.42
priority to clearly-attackable. hb-093 (per-position residual SNR gate)
becomes attractive because it cuts work in the back-half of decoding.

**Defensible prior.** WSJT-X-Improved a8 mode explicitly markets "0.5-1s
early decode" as the headline feature — TTFD is what they're optimizing.
For autonomous TX scheduling, the marginal QSO value of a decode at T+9s
vs T+14s is the difference between "tx in the next slot" and "defer 30s."

**Assumption challenged.** "Recall by end-of-slot is the same as recall
in time to act on it."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Yes, measurable on existing corpus — pancetta-ft8
already has decode-emit ordering. Need to instrument `Decoder::decode_pass`
to emit per-decode timestamps. ~50 LOC harness change, no production
change.

**Effort.** 1 session — instrument + run on hard-200, compare hypothesis
ranking.

**Headline risk.** Gameable by early FP emissions. Must combine with
precision gate.

## M2 — Operational-value-weighted recall

**Definition.** Weight each truth decode by its **operator value** before
summing:

```
opv_recall = Σ_truths (value(callsign, grid) × decoded?) / Σ_truths value(callsign, grid)
```

`value(callsign, grid)` = priority score from `pancetta-qso/src/priority.rs`
evaluated against K5ARH's worked-set (needed-DXCC, needed-grid, POTA/SOTA,
rarity). Common-callsign decodes count near 0; rare-DXCC decodes count near
1.0. Average dominated by tail.

**Why this would change behavior.** Recall optimizations that pull in 50
extra US callsigns score near-zero. Optimizations that pull in one extra
JA, KH6, or 3Y0J score near 1.0. Suddenly hb-064 (DIA-OSD: -135 novels at
+5.3× per-bit precision, currently shelved at Session 2) may *win* if
those 135 novels are common US calls and a smaller number of rare DX comes
through. Inverts which novels matter.

**Defensible prior.** Direct match to operator behavior. K5ARH would
choose a decoder that gives him +1 JA/day over +50 US/day. The priority
scorer is already production code; the metric is a re-aggregation of
existing signal.

**Assumption challenged.** "Every recovered decode is worth 1/N."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Yes, measurable today. Needs cqdx.io rarity
lookup for offline scoring (or a snapshotted rarity table) — both exist.
Compute value-weighted recall on hard-200 truths, compare to plain recall;
if Spearman correlation > 0.95, the metric collapses to plain recall and
is uninteresting. If correlation < 0.7, hypothesis ranking will diverge.

**Effort.** 2 sessions: 1 to plumb rarity lookup into the eval harness, 1
to run on hard-200 + corpus and inspect hypothesis re-ranking.

**Headline risk.** Distorts decoder evaluation toward "DX bias" — may
mask real recall regressions on US calls that operators *do* still want
to log. Mitigate with floor weight (every truth gets ≥ 0.1).

## M3 — QSO-completion-rate on simulated rig loop

**Definition.** Replay each hard-200 slot through a full
`encode→modulate→decode→state-machine→tx-scheduler→decode-next-slot`
loop with a simulated DX responder. Score = fraction of attempted QSOs
that complete a CQ→grid→report→RR73 exchange end-to-end. Compute as
average over N replayed sessions.

**Why this would change behavior.** A decode that arrives but with wrong
DT (>15Hz off, or >2.5s late) under M3 fails to advance the QSO state
machine. M3 measures *actionable* decodes, not raw decodes. Changes
which precision-vs-recall tradeoff wins: a decoder that recovers 90% of
QSO-relevant exchanges beats one that recovers 95% of decodes but
mis-extracts callsign/grid for 10% of exchanges. Closely related but
strictly more demanding than M1.

**Defensible prior.** Pancetta's *purpose* per CLAUDE.md is "decode, call,
complete QSOs, and log." The composite metric should measure what the
project ships.

**Assumption challenged.** "A decoded message text is as good as a
completed QSO."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Pancetta already has loopback QSO tests
(`pancetta/tests/loopback_qso`). Extension: drive the loopback test from
the hard-200 corpus, substituting each truth callsign as the DX. Estimate
~500 LOC for the replay harness. Kill-switch: if Pearson(M3, recall) >
0.95 across 20 sweep configs, M3 is redundant; abandon.

**Effort.** 3 sessions — replay infra is non-trivial; QSO state machine
already exists.

**Headline risk.** Compute-expensive (full loop per slot). Distorted by
state-machine sequencer config (`is_message_relevant` tolerances).

## M4 — Precision–recall AUC with FP-injection

**Definition.** Augment the eval corpus with N synthetic "garbage" slots
(pure noise + adversarial near-Costas patterns). Compute precision =
true-positives / (true-positives + false-positives), recall = current
metric. Score = trapezoidal AUC of the PR curve as `min_sync_score` and
`max_parity_errors_for_osd` are swept across their config ranges.

**Why this would change behavior.** Today the FP filter is a binary
gate — `fp_filter_active: true`. Iters that erode precision but raise
recall pass as long as FP-rate stays at zero on the curated corpus.
M4 measures *robustness* of precision under stress. hb-052 (production
FP filter) gains explicit credit. Wild-50 (0/96 decode rate today) likely
reflects FP risk, not pure recall failure — M4 would surface that.

**Defensible prior.** PR-AUC is standard for binary classification under
imbalanced labels. FT8 decode-or-not is exactly that.

**Assumption challenged.** "Zero FPs on the curated corpus = production-safe."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Need to build an FP-injection corpus (~200 slots
of synthetic garbage + intentional Costas-pattern decoys). Can re-use
synth-clean generator with target_text injection at non-Costas symbol
positions. If injection PR-AUC tracks plain recall (Pearson > 0.95), the
metric reveals nothing new. If decoder shows precision cliff below
some sync-score threshold, the metric is load-bearing.

**Effort.** 1 session to build FP-injection corpus + measure.

**Headline risk.** FP-injection corpus is synthetic — may not represent
real-world FPs (e.g., contest QRM, splatter, image responses on rig).

## M5 — Saturation-aware composite (corpus-shift-robust)

**Definition.** Replace `decode_rate(hard-200)` with a **rank-normalized**
score: `rank(decoder, all_decoders_history) / len(decoders_history)`. Or,
simpler: `composite = old_composite − k × |composite_change_explained_by_corpus_shift|`,
where corpus shift is estimated by running the previous main HEAD's
decoder on the current corpus and computing the delta. Equivalent to:

```
score' = score(current_decoder, current_corpus) − score(prev_main, current_corpus) + score(prev_main, prev_corpus)
```

— so when the corpus rotates (new hard-200), the metric stays comparable
to the previous baseline.

**Why this would change behavior.** Today, the corpus-survey says we
need to refresh hard-200 (composite will discontinuously jump). Under M5,
the refresh is *automatic* — every iter's score is measured against a
stable virtual baseline. Long-running graduation tracking ("cumulative
+0.013993 over 5 days") survives corpus refreshes. Removes the pressure
to delay corpus refresh.

**Defensible prior.** Standard practice in any benchmark that evolves
(SQuAD → SQuAD 2.0 normalization, ImageNet test-set rotation).

**Assumption challenged.** "Composite numbers are comparable across
corpus versions."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Requires storing prev_main decoder binary or
re-running it on demand. Computable from existing infrastructure — the
research harness already keeps `main` baseline scorecards.

**Effort.** 1 session — pure aggregation change; no decoder change.

**Headline risk.** Hides real recall growth — if today's decoder is 5%
better but corpus is 5% harder, M5 reports 0% change, which is honest
but unsatisfying.

## M6 — Per-band-density-stratified recall

**Definition.** Bucket each WAV into one of {sparse, medium, dense} by
pancetta_decode_count (e.g., < 20, 20–30, > 30). Report recall per
bucket; composite weight per bucket = empirical fraction of K5ARH's
operating slots in that bucket from the most recent 7-day capture.

```
score = Σ_buckets P(bucket | recent_capture) × recall(bucket | hard-200)
```

**Why this would change behavior.** hb-086 V1 (joint-pair-retry) wins on
dense slots; hb-079 wins everywhere. Stratification surfaces which iter
targets which regime. The current monolithic recall obscures whether a
+12 rec/hard-200 is +12 on dense slots (where joint-pair lives) or +12
spread thin. Strongly redirects which novels are worth a graduation.

**Defensible prior.** Standard stratification for any sample where
sub-populations have different intrinsic difficulty. WSJT-X release notes
often describe wins by SNR bucket, not aggregate.

**Assumption challenged.** "The hard-200 distribution matches the
operator's distribution."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Yes, fully measurable from existing scorecards
(pancetta_decode_count is per-WAV). Re-aggregate today's results.

**Effort.** 1 session — pure post-processing.

**Headline risk.** Bucket boundaries are arbitrary; results sensitive to
binning. Mitigate by reporting both quartiles and the weighted sum.

## M7 — CPU-cost-adjusted recall (decodes / joule, or / second)

**Definition.** `score = decodes_recovered / elapsed_seconds` (or per
joule with `powermetrics` on macOS / `rapl` on Linux). Normalize against
a baseline run of jt9 on the same corpus on the same hardware:
`score_norm = (decodes/s)_pancetta / (decodes/s)_jt9_baseline`.

**Why this would change behavior.** Multipass = 3× decode time. Joint-pair
on residual = +2.2% elapsed (this is already tracked in journals).
Cumulative cost across 27 graduations is unmeasured. M7 turns elapsed-time
deltas into a first-class composite term. hb-093 (skip noise positions
early) becomes the highest-priority hypothesis. Wild-card hb-094
(autoencoder pre-LDPC) potentially regresses heavily.

**Defensible prior.** Pancetta's target hardware is a MiniPC running
Windows 11. Field-deployed FT8 stations are CPU-constrained. WSJT-X
itself ships separate `decoder_count` settings to bound CPU.

**Assumption challenged.** "Elapsed time within +5% is free."

**Kill-switch sketch.** `elapsed_seconds` already in scorecard
(`harness.elapsed_seconds`). Power measurement requires
`powermetrics --samplers cpu_power -o ...` on macOS or `s-tui`/`rapl` on
Linux. For a cheap first pass, use elapsed-seconds proxy and skip joules.

**Effort.** 2 sessions: 1 for elapsed proxy and integration, +1 for
joule measurement if needed.

**Headline risk.** Discourages all algorithmic recall improvements.
Mitigate with a Pareto-aware composite that penalizes only Pareto-dominated
configs.

## M8 — First-derivative recall (Δ-recall vs main per iter)

**Definition.** Don't measure absolute recall. Measure the **per-iter
delta**: `score = (recall_iter − recall_main) / hours_since_last_graduation`.
Composite over the project = integral of Δ-recall over time.

**Why this would change behavior.** Reframes the headline question from
"is the decoder good?" to "is the project making progress?" Each iter's
value is its **rate of recall growth**. Plateaus become explicit (a week
of +0.0001 deltas is visible as a near-zero integral). Forces strategic
question: when to refresh the corpus, when to switch from decoder work
to operational work (Phase 5 autonomous QSO loop).

**Defensible prior.** Standard project-velocity metric in any benchmark
research. "Time to plateau" is a defined concept in BO/active learning
literature.

**Assumption challenged.** "Iter quality is independent of project state."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Yes, measurable — all graduation timestamps and
deltas are in `research/scorecards/journal.md` (or equivalent). Pure
post-processing.

**Effort.** 1 session — produce a velocity dashboard.

**Headline risk.** Discourages compound wins. A 3-iter sequence each
yielding +0.001 looks worse than a single +0.003 even though
contributions sum. Maybe report both integral *and* peak.

## M9 — Adversarial-corpus recall (jt9-only-hits subset)

**Definition.** Build a corpus from the corpus-survey's
"jt9-only/slot = 8.7" finding: only the 174 slot-decodes that jt9 found
and pancetta missed on 2026-05-30. Score = recall on this subset.
Continuously updated: every graduation that closes the gap reduces the
subset; new captures replenish it.

**Why this would change behavior.** Targets the **30% real recall
headroom** identified in the corpus survey directly. Forces every iter
to compete on the hardest 30% — the part where pancetta is *worse* than
jt9, not the part where it's already 123.7%. Closes the "curated by our
own decoder" loop.

**Defensible prior.** Standard adversarial benchmarking — train on
where you fail. Used in any active-learning system.

**Assumption challenged.** "The hard-200 sample represents pancetta's
failure modes." It doesn't — hard-200 was curated *by* pancetta, so it
contains the subset where pancetta does decode (otherwise it wouldn't be
visible to the curation step). M9 inverts that.

**Kill-switch sketch.** Yes, fully measurable. Re-run today's 20-WAV
survey on the full 2066-WAV captures to enumerate all jt9-only decodes;
freeze that list as `adversarial_174.manifest.json`. ~3-5 hours compute
on existing hardware.

**Effort.** 1 session to build the manifest + integrate into harness.

**Headline risk.** Subset shrinks as decoder improves (success → metric
disappears). Need refresh policy.

## M10 — DXCC-coverage rate (unique entities/hr) [WILD]

**Definition.** Score = `unique_DXCC_entities_in_decoded_calls / hours_of_recording`.
Operationally meaningful: it's literally what a contester or DX-hunter
optimizes. Measurable on real captures; not measurable on synthetic
slots.

**Why this would change behavior.** Eliminates the per-slot recall game
entirely. A decoder that catches a JA once is worth more than the same
decoder catching 50 US callsigns. Strongly correlated with M2 but at
session granularity, not slot granularity. Makes wild-100 (full session
captures) the headline corpus; demotes hard-200 to a regression-guard
fixture.

**Defensible prior.** Direct match to operator behavior. DXCC count is
the actual scoring system of the hobby.

**Assumption challenged.** "Per-slot recall scales to per-session
operator value."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Requires DXCC mapping (callsign → DXCC entity).
cqdx.io has this; offline snapshot is feasible. Run on wild-100 +
upcoming wild-1000-style capture. If DXCC-coverage rate correlates 1:1
with raw decode rate across decoders, M10 is redundant.

**Effort.** 2 sessions — DXCC mapping plumbing + measurement.

**Headline risk.** Strongly diurnal — same decoder scores wildly
different on dawn-gray-line vs midday-deep-noise. Mitigate with rolling
24h windows.

## M11 — Information-theoretic recall (Shannon-weighted bits/slot) [WILD]

**Definition.** Each recovered FT8 message is 77 bits of payload + 14
CRC + 83 LDPC parity. Score = `total_recovered_payload_bits / slot_seconds
× p(rare_message)`. Weight by Shannon information: a rare callsign or
unusual grid carries more bits-of-surprise per the empirical distribution
on the corpus. Score = `Σ_decoded log₂(1 / p(message))`.

**Why this would change behavior.** A decoder that recovers 100 instances
of "CQ K1JT FN42" gets less credit than one that recovers a single "CQ
ZL7AC RE79" — because the second is 12+ bits of surprise vs 4 bits.
Direct information-theoretic measure of *novelty*. Lines up with
operator-value but on a defensible mathematical basis (no human-tuned
weights like in M2).

**Defensible prior.** Cross-entropy / KL-divergence are standard in any
classification benchmark with imbalanced classes. WSJT-X's own discussion
of "rare callsign" in DXpedition mode is implicitly this.

**Assumption challenged.** "Decode count is a measure of decoder value."
No — *decode information content* is.

**Kill-switch sketch.** Requires an empirical message distribution.
Build from cqdx.io's 1-year spot history (~10⁸ spots) → `p(callsign)` ≈
spot_count / total_spots. Compute Shannon weights offline; apply to
hard-200 truths. Pearson(M11, plain recall) > 0.9 = uninteresting.

**Effort.** 1 session if cqdx.io spot histogram is available; +1 for
infra if not.

**Headline risk.** Abstract. Hard to translate "decoder X gains 14.7
bits/slot vs decoder Y" into "X is better." Pair with M10 for grounded
interpretation.

## M12 — Counterfactual-impact: "how many QSOs would have completed?" [WILD]

**Definition.** For each historical slot in an 8h K5ARH session, replay
through pancetta with the candidate decoder + autonomous operator config.
Count QSO completions vs the *same* replay with main-branch decoder.
Score = `completed_qsos(candidate) − completed_qsos(main)`. Aggregate
over N replayed sessions (target N=10 to get a stable estimate).

**Why this would change behavior.** Reduces all the indirect proxies to
the single operational outcome that matters. A decoder that helps complete
1 extra QSO/hour is worth a graduation regardless of any micro-recall
delta. Conversely, an iter that adds 10 decodes/slot but those decodes
don't change which CQs the operator answers — score = 0.

**Defensible prior.** This is the **counterfactual treatment effect**
from causal inference. Standard for A/B-testing systems where the action
distribution depends on the input.

**Assumption challenged.** "Recall improvements automatically translate
to operator outcomes."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Requires:
(1) replay-able QSO state machine (mostly exists),
(2) simulated DX responder that follows real-world response statistics,
(3) variance estimate per replayed session.
~3 sessions of infra build, then the metric is cheap to compute.

**Effort.** 4 sessions total (infra-heavy).

**Headline risk.** High variance per session. Need many replays per
config; expensive. Also tightly coupled to autonomous-config (rerank
when priority weights change). Probably the **right** metric long-term
but a heavy lift.

## M13 — Cross-decoder-disagreement recall

**Definition.** Subset = `{truth decodes that jt9 misses AND jtdx misses
AND wsjt-x-improved misses}`. Score = recall on this set. Numerator is
strictly bounded by "decodes no other reference decoder finds."

**Why this would change behavior.** Drives the project toward
*unique-value* — what does pancetta do that no other decoder does?
Currently the scorecard reports `wsjtx_decoded` and `jtdx_decoded` but
the composite ignores them. M13 prioritizes precisely the gap where
ensemble (hb-028, wild-card) can't help. Strongly distinguishes hb-079
(coherent multipass) wins from baseline-equivalent wins.

**Defensible prior.** Software diversity literature — coverage of "what
only this implementation finds" is a standard quality measure.

**Assumption challenged.** "Matching jt9 is the goal." Maybe *exceeding*
jt9+jtdx+wsjt-x-improved is the goal.

**Kill-switch sketch.** Needs jtdx and wsjt-x-improved baselines on the
corpus. jt9 we have; jtdx is a separate binary; wsjt-x-improved is a
separate binary. ~2 days compute to baseline 1000 WAVs across 3
decoders. Manifest + cache from there.

**Effort.** 1 session for the metric; infra cost is corpus baselining,
which is one-time.

**Headline risk.** Small absolute numbers (the set is small by
construction). Hard to drive a hill-climb against. Use as a tracking
metric, not the headline.

## M14 — Listen-only Pareto: (recall, latency, CPU%) Pareto frontier [WILD]

**Definition.** Reject the idea of a single composite. Instead, define a
**3D Pareto frontier** over (recall, TTFD-latency, CPU%). A decoder
config is "Pareto-dominated" if some other config beats it on all three
axes simultaneously. Score = membership/distance to the current frontier.

**Why this would change behavior.** Removes the single-scalar tyranny.
Forces every graduation to make explicit which axis it sacrifices.
hb-079 (recall++, latency--, CPU--) gets through if it's Pareto-optimal,
not because we pre-decided recall dominates. Surfaces config-level
choices: "main is high-recall low-latency; minipc is high-recall
mid-latency low-CPU; raspberry-pi is mid-recall low-latency
ultra-low-CPU."

**Defensible prior.** Standard in any multi-objective optimization
literature (NSGA-II, etc.). Used in compiler benchmarks, ML inference
optimization.

**Assumption challenged.** "Composite scores can be linearly weighted."

**Kill-switch sketch.** Measurable today (M1 + M7 + recall). Output is a
3D scatter, not a scalar — UI/workflow change.

**Effort.** 2 sessions — instrumentation + visualization, plus
re-think of "graduation" criteria (frontier-membership instead of
composite-delta).

**Headline risk.** No single number = harder to communicate, harder to
diff iters. Probably best as a *complement* to a scalar composite, not
a replacement.

## M15 — Operator-day-replay composite (8h K5ARH session) [WILD, FLAGSHIP]

**Definition.** Flagship synthesis of M2 + M3 + M10 + M12. The composite
metric IS: replay the most-recent 8h K5ARH operating session through the
full pancetta pipeline (decode → priority-score → schedule → tx →
state-machine), with a simulated DX responder fed from real spot
statistics. Composite score = `(NEW_DXCC_worked × 1.0) + (NEW_grids ×
0.5) + (POTA_qsos × 0.4) + (all_other_qsos × 0.1)` per the priority
weights in `pancetta-qso/src/priority.rs`. Reported in
"operator-points/day."

**Why this would change behavior.** Every other metric on this list is
*subsumed*. Recall, latency, precision, CPU-cost all affect the final
score through their effect on what QSOs complete. The composite is
*literally what the project ships*. Hypothesis ranking would be radically
reshuffled: decoder wins that don't propagate to QSO outcomes (most of
them, if M12's variance is high) drop in priority; QSO-loop wins (a8,
TX scheduling, priority weighting) move up.

**Defensible prior.** End-to-end task evaluation is the gold standard in
any system that has a clear downstream task. NLP moved from BLEU →
downstream-task accuracy for the same reason. Pancetta's downstream task
is "complete QSOs to maximize operator points."

**Assumption challenged.** ALL of them. This is the metric the project's
goal statement implies, vs the metric we currently optimize.

**Kill-switch sketch.** Massive infra build:
- replay harness for 8h sessions (24× 1920 slots),
- simulated DX-station responder fed from cqdx-style empirical statistics,
- operator-config that encodes K5ARH's needed-DXCC/grid sets,
- variance estimator (one 8h replay ≈ 1 sample; need many for confidence).
Estimated 5+ sessions to build before first measurement.

**Effort.** 5 sessions — biggest single metric infra investment in the
project.

**Headline risk.** Three:
(1) variance per replay too high to compare iters;
(2) the DX responder simulation is the bottleneck and only as good as
    its parametrization;
(3) replays take hours, slowing the per-iter feedback loop.
But: even if M15 is too heavy as a hill-climb metric, it's the *right*
**quarterly progress report** metric. Worth building as a "monthly

## Cross-cutting observations

1. **Three clusters emerge among the 15 ideas:**
   - *Operational-value-weighting* (M2, M10, M11, M15) — different ways
     to score what an operator cares about.
   - *Latency/cost-axis* (M1, M7, M14) — what the composite ignores.
   - *Corpus/measurement-quality* (M5, M6, M8, M9, M13) — how we know
     the metric isn't fooling us.
   - *End-to-end* (M3, M12, M15) — the QSO-completion family, ranging
     from "1 session of infra" (M3) to "5 sessions" (M15).

2. **M5 is the highest-immediate-leverage idea.** Saturation-aware
   composite would let the corpus refresh happen without losing the
   cumulative graduation log. It's 1 session of work, no decoder
   change, and it unblocks the corpus refresh the survey already
   recommended. This is the metric "definitely worth shipping."

3. **M9 is the highest-recall-leverage idea.** Adversarial corpus
   (174 jt9-only decodes from the survey) directly targets the 30%
   real headroom. It's already partially scoped in the survey's
   recommendation. 1 session of work.

4. **M1 is the highest-operational-leverage cheap idea.** TTFD measurement
   would re-rank hb-091 (a8) up, re-rank hb-079 (multipass) down, and
   instrument a real axis the project ignores. 1 session.

5. **M15 is the most-likely-to-change-strategy idea, but at 5 sessions
   of cost.** Worth a long-horizon spawn even if not immediately
   prioritized. The end-to-end QSO replay is the project's true
   measurement target.
